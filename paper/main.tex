\begin{abstract}
Liquid-cooled artificial intelligence (AI) data centers couple a large,
controllable cooling load to megajoules of thermal mass, yet no existing
scheme lets an operator \emph{commit} this flexibility in a day-ahead market:
the deliverable depth moves with humidity, workload, and thermal state, and a
commitment that cannot be honored becomes either a reliability penalty or a
silicon-temperature excursion. In this paper, we propose ENCORE, an
envelope-certified offering method for grid-interactive liquid cooling.
ENCORE characterizes the set of deliverable (offer, duration) pairs exactly as
a context-dependent polyhedron under joint hotspot and anti-condensation
constraints, with a closed-form weather-coupled virtual-battery equivalent.
Two nested context-conditional disturbance sets robustify the envelope
offline: a safety set that is never traded for revenue, and a delivery set
whose residual risk the settlement prices. A certified fallback policy then
underwrites a deliberately simple real-time controller. We prove that any
offer inside the tightened envelope satisfies thermal safety on the safety
set and meets its settled energy obligation at confidence $1-\epsd$. A case
study built entirely on measured data (a 2.0-million-worker GPU-cluster
trace, archived day-ahead weather forecasts, and ten 2024 ERCOT weeks)
validates both clauses out of sample, with zero in-domain violation episodes
across $1{,}400$ controller-days. Day-ahead workload information alone raises a real hall's
certifiable offer from $40.7$ to $74.6$\,kW per MW of IT load, and the
certified participant earns \$$97$/day per MW during a scarcity week with zero
capital expenditure and zero impact on compute workloads.
\end{abstract}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This paper formulated cooling-side flexibility of liquid-cooled AI data
centers as a day-ahead offering problem and developed ENCORE, which certifies
the deliverable (offer, duration) set as a context-dependent polyhedral
envelope and offers from its robustified interior under nested
safety/delivery disturbance sets. The certificate survives from commitment to
real-time control: across ten measured ERCOT weeks, the certified participant
incurred zero violation episodes within its stated domain while delivery
failures remained inside the priced $\epsd$ budget, against $229$
violation-days for the uncertified envelope. Day-ahead workload information
raised the certifiable offer of a real GPU hall by $83\%$, and scarcity-week
value reached \$$97$/day per MW of IT load with no added hardware. Cooling-side
thermal inertia can therefore be sold, not merely demonstrated: certified
envelopes make the loop a market resource while keeping hardware safety
constraints intact.
