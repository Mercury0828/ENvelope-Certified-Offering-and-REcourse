\title{Certified Cooling-Side Flexibility for Liquid-Cooled AI Data Centers:
Context-Dependent Envelopes with End-to-End Delivery Guarantees}

\author{Jiachen~Shen% <-- author block to be completed by owner
\thanks{Manuscript received \todo{date}. The author is with \todo{affiliation}.}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The thermal inertia of a liquid-cooled data center is a fast, capex-free source
of grid flexibility, but no existing scheme lets an operator \emph{commit} it in
a day-ahead market: the deliverable depth depends on weather, workload, and the
plant's thermal state, and a commitment that cannot be honored becomes either a
reliability penalty or a silicon-temperature excursion. This paper introduces
the \emph{certified flexibility envelope}: an exact polyhedral characterization
of the set of (offer, duration) pairs a liquid-cooled facility can deliver
under joint hotspot and condensation constraints, parameterized by a context
vector of dew-point and workload forecasts, with a closed-form weather-coupled
virtual-battery equivalent. A two-stage scheme offers from a robustified
envelope: nested context-conditional disturbance sets --- a safety set that is
never traded for revenue and a delivery set whose excess risk the settlement
prices --- tighten the envelope offline, and a certified fallback policy
underwrites a deliberately simple real-time controller. Any offer inside the
tightened envelope satisfies thermal safety on the safety set and a delivery
guarantee at a chosen confidence on the delivery set. A case study built
entirely on measured data --- a 2.0-million-worker GPU-cluster trace, archived
day-ahead weather forecasts, and ten 2024 ERCOT weeks --- validates both
clauses out of sample (zero in-domain violations in 1{,}400 controller-days)
and quantifies the product: day-ahead workload information alone moves a real
hall's certifiable offer from 41 to 75\,kW per MW of IT load, and the
certified participant earns \$97/day per MW during scarcity weeks with zero
capital expenditure and zero impact on compute.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{A}{rtificial-intelligence} data centers are the fastest-growing
load on many power systems, and the grid is beginning to demand flexibility in
return for interconnection. Texas Senate Bill~6 (2025) subjects large loads to
curtailment-readiness requirements~\cite{sb6}; Google has signed demand-response
agreements covering on the order of a gigawatt of data-center load~\cite{googledr};
and a recent assessment finds that modest flexibility from large new loads
would unlock interconnection capacity for tens of gigawatts in existing U.S.
systems~\cite{norris25}. The supply side of this bargain is forming on the
\emph{compute} side: a field demonstration in Phoenix reduced a production AI
cluster's power by 25\% for three hours by orchestrating workloads
\cite{colangelo25}, and workload-level studies report 18--55\% power
flexibility across AI job types~\cite{acun26}.

This paper develops the complementary, untapped resource: the \emph{cooling
side} of the new liquid-cooled AI facility. Direct liquid cooling concentrates
30--40\% of facility power in a chilled-water plant coupled to megajoules of
coolant thermal mass; measurements on a production liquid-cooled hall show
that relaxing the supply temperature from 17 to 25\,$^\circ$C sheds 63\% of
cooling power~\cite{gheni26}. Field tests a decade ago already exercised
cooling-side demand response in air-cooled facilities~\cite{ghatikar12}, and
co-controlling chilled-water setpoints with server power yields measurable
regulation capacity~\cite{fu20}. Thermal inertia is real, fast, and --- unlike
batteries or workload curtailment --- already paid for.

What is missing is the step from \emph{potential} to \emph{product}. A
day-ahead market participant must commit a deliverable quantity hours before
delivery, yet the cooling plant's deliverable depth is not a number: it is a
set that shrinks with humidity (a condensation floor on supply temperature
binds in humid climates), with workload volatility (bursts consume the same
thermal buffer the product sells), and with the duration the product must
sustain. Committing the deterministic maximum invites delivery failures and
hotspot excursions; committing a static worst case forfeits most of the value.
The object that resolves this --- the analogue of a do-not-exceed
limit~\cite{zhao15} or a dynamic operating envelope~\cite{aemo22} for a
thermal resource whose limits move with the weather --- has not been
constructed.

We construct it. The contributions are three:

\emph{C1 (Object).} The \textbf{certified flexibility envelope}: an exact
polyhedral characterization of the deliverable (offer $q$, duration $d$) set
of a liquid-cooled facility under joint hotspot and condensation constraints,
parameterized by a context vector (dew-point and workload forecasts), with a
closed-form \emph{weather-coupled virtual battery} equivalent --- a battery
whose capacity is a function of the weather (Sec.~\ref{sec:envelope}).

\emph{C2 (Method).} A \textbf{two-stage offering scheme with end-to-end
guarantees}: day-ahead offers constrained to an envelope tightened by
\emph{nested} context-conditional disturbance sets --- a safety set, never
traded for revenue, and a delivery set whose excess risk the settlement
prices --- and a real-time layer in which a certified fallback policy
underwrites a deliberately simple controller. Theorem~\ref{thm:main}: any
offer inside the tightened envelope satisfies thermal safety for all
disturbances in the safety set and meets its settled energy obligation at
confidence $1-\epsd$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:offering}).

\emph{C3 (Evidence).} A \textbf{fully measured-data case study} --- GPU-cluster
workload trace, archived day-ahead weather forecasts, ten seasons-spanning 2024
ERCOT weeks --- that validates both theorem clauses out of sample and
quantifies the economics, including a finding with direct operational meaning:
day-ahead \emph{workload information} (the operator's own running-job state)
nearly doubles the certifiable offer of a real hall
(Sec.~\ref{sec:casestudy}).

This work descends from the buildings-to-grid reserve literature
\cite{vrettos16tse,vrettos16tps,gorecki17} and differs where the resource
does: the feasible set's lower boundary is coupled to an exogenous stochastic
variable (dew point) rather than a static comfort band; loop dynamics are
minutes-scale against seconds-scale GPU heat bursts rather than hours-scale
building mass; and the certified-envelope object itself, rather than a
reserve schedule, is the deliverable. Relative to a companion work on
distributionally robust \emph{control} of the same plant class (E2E-CDRO,
under review~\cite{e2ecdro}), the present paper solves the \emph{offering}
problem: the decision object is a market commitment with a certificate;
robustness acts offline as constraint tightening of an envelope rather than
online in a min--sup controller; and the market layer (product, settlement,
penalties) is part of the model rather than absent.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\emph{Data centers as grid resources.} Surveys and system studies established
schedulable IT load as a flexibility source~\cite{takci25,wierman14}; recent
work demonstrates it at production scale on AI clusters
\cite{colangelo25,acun26}. All act on compute. Early field studies
\cite{ghatikar12} exercised cooling-side DR in air-cooled facilities
(2--8-min chiller/CRAC response, up to 25\% site load in tests), and
\cite{fu20} co-adjusted server frequency and chilled-water supply temperature
for frequency regulation. These quantify potential; none defines a committable
product with a delivery guarantee, which is the gap this paper fills.

\emph{Liquid-cooled thermal flexibility.} Measurements on production
liquid-cooled halls quantify large, fast cooling-power modulation through
supply-temperature control~\cite{gheni26}. Thermal-storage retrofits ---
reusable thermal energy storage~\cite{oh25}, Carnot batteries~\cite{zhang25},
heat-prosumer integration~\cite{ravi26} --- add hardware to monetize heat. We
differ structurally: the loop itself, unmodified, is the resource; a buffer
tank appears in our framework only as a sensitivity parameter.

\emph{Committing flexible load day-ahead.} Bi-level bidding for data centers
\cite{chen23} and day-ahead workload commitment with deliverability
constraints~\cite{fanzhao26} establish the commitment-and-deliverability
frame for the compute side; we adopt the frame and change the resource, which
changes the mathematics (state-dependent input sets, weather-coupled
constraints, thermal dynamics).

\emph{Certifying the maximum offer.} Our envelope is the thermal-resource
member of a family: do-not-exceed limits for variable resources
\cite{zhao15}, dynamic operating envelopes for distribution-connected DER
\cite{aemo22}, aggregate virtual batteries for thermostatic loads
\cite{hao15}, flexibility functions~\cite{junker18}, and policy-based
reserves~\cite{warrington13}. The buildings-to-grid line
\cite{vrettos16tse,vrettos16tps,gorecki17} provides the methodological
template of offline robust capacity allocation under a simple online layer;
Sec.~\ref{sec:intro} lists the three structural departures. Within this
family, the combination of an \emph{exact} deliverability polyhedron, a
context-conditional (conformal) uncertainty description, and a settlement
that prices the residual delivery risk is, to our knowledge, new.

\section{Problem Setting}\label{sec:setting}

\subsection{Timescales and Product}
A facility operator offers, at day-ahead closure, an hourly pair $(q_h, d)$:
\emph{if activated, reduce cooling power by up to $q_h$ (kW) below baseline,
sustainable for up to $d$ minutes within hour $h$}. The duration
$d \in \{15, 30, 60\}$\,min is a product parameter fixed per market (we
evaluate $d=30$); activation is exogenous with probability $p_{\mathrm{act}}$
and activated fraction $r_h \in [0,1]$, $r_h \cdot 60 \le d$. The product is
stylized --- it maps loosely onto short-duration reserve and emergency
products without encoding any single tariff --- and it is the \emph{only}
institutional element in the model; all other market realism (prices, weather,
workload) enters through data. On the operating day a rolling controller acts
every $\Delta = 5$\,min within market intervals of $\dH = 1$\,h; sub-minute
chip-level dynamics enter through intra-interval peak statistics
(Sec.~\ref{sec:plant}).

\subsection{Settlement}\label{sec:settlement}
Hour $h$ with commitment $q_h$ and activation $r_h$ settles on metered
cooling power $P_t$ against an exogenous baseline $P^{\mathrm{b}}$:
revenue $\pi^{\mathrm{cap}}_h q_h \dH$; delivered energy
$\sum_{t \in h} (P^{\mathrm{b}} - P_t)\Delta$ accumulated over the
\emph{whole hour}; shortfall
$s_h = [\,r_h q_h \dH - \textstyle\sum_{t\in h}(P^{\mathrm{b}}-P_t)\Delta\,]_+$
penalized at $\gamma\,\pi^{\mathrm{cap}}_h$ per unit energy with
$\gamma \ge 1$; real-time energy deviations settle at the RT price; and a
degradation cost $c_{\mathrm{deg}} \int [T_{\mathrm{j}}(t) - T_{\mathrm{thr}}]_+\,dt$
prices sustained hotspot elevation. Whole-hour metering is load-bearing:
recovery \emph{within} the activated hour cancels delivered energy, so a
certificate that only guarantees the activation window over-promises; the
envelope of Sec.~\ref{sec:envelope} certifies both the window depth and the
hour energy.

\begin{remark}[Baseline]\label{rem:baseline}
The baseline is exogenous and known (the no-participation cooling trajectory
under the frozen-efficiency surrogate of Sec.~\ref{sec:plant}). Baseline
manipulation is a real phenomenon in DR programs~\cite{chao11}; it is
orthogonal to deliverability and excluded by construction here, as in the
buildings-reserves literature.
\end{remark}

\section{Plant Model}\label{sec:plant}

\subsection{Thermal Dynamics with a Virtual Input}
We model the thermal path of a 1\,MW$_{\mathrm{IT}}$ liquid-cooled hall by
lumped energy balances. States are the hotspot proxy $T_{\mathrm{j}}$
(silicon-adjacent metal), the coolant-loop temperature $T_{\mathrm{w}}$, and
--- in the facility-loop configuration used throughout the experiments --- the
facility-water temperature $T_{\mathrm{f}}$:
\begin{align}
C_{\mathrm{j}} \dot T_{\mathrm{j}} &= Q_{\mathrm{IT}}(t)
  - h_{\mathrm{jw}}\,(T_{\mathrm{j}} - T_{\mathrm{w}}), \notag\\
C_{\mathrm{w}} \dot T_{\mathrm{w}} &= h_{\mathrm{jw}}\,(T_{\mathrm{j}} -
  T_{\mathrm{w}}) - u_{\mathrm{ext}}, \label{eq:plant}\\
C_{\mathrm{f}} \dot T_{\mathrm{f}} &= u_{\mathrm{ext}} - u_{\mathrm{rej}},
\notag
\end{align}
where $Q_{\mathrm{IT}} = \bar Q + w(t)$ is the IT heat load with residual $w$,
$u_{\mathrm{ext}}$ is the heat extracted from the loop, and $u_{\mathrm{rej}}$
the heat rejected by the chilled-water plant. The pair
$u = (u_{\mathrm{ext}}, u_{\mathrm{rej}})$ is a \emph{virtual input}: it
absorbs the operator's actual handles (supply temperature setpoint, flow) into
heat-rate variables, with the realization layer mapping back to setpoints.
This renders the dynamics linear time-invariant; all nonlinearity is confined
to the state-dependent input set
\begin{align}
\mathcal{U}(x; c) = \Big\{ u :\;
& 0 \le u_{\mathrm{ext}} \le \dot m c_p \big(T_{\mathrm{w}} -
  \vartheta(c)\big),\; \notag\\
& u_{\mathrm{ext}} \le \dot m_{\mathrm{f}} c_p (T_{\mathrm{w}} -
  T_{\mathrm{f}} - \delta_{\mathrm{hx}}),\;
 0 \le u_{\mathrm{rej}} \le \bar u_{\mathrm{rej}} \Big\},
\label{eq:Ux}
\end{align}
which is affine in $x$ for fixed context --- no bilinear terms survive. The
first bound carries the model's signature constraint: the supply-temperature
floor $\vartheta(c) = \max\{\vartheta_{\min},\, \Tdew + \delta_{\mathrm{c}}\}$
sits above the dew point to preclude condensation, so \emph{humidity directly
truncates the input set}. Safety requires $T_{\mathrm{j}} \le T^{\max}$ and
$T_{\mathrm{f}} \le T_{\mathrm{f}}^{\max}$ at every step. Cooling power under
the frozen-efficiency surrogate is affine in the rejection rate,
$P = P_0 + u_{\mathrm{rej}}/\eta$, which makes power constraints linear; the
surrogate freezes the weather-dependence of plant efficiency within the hour
(its drift enters the degradation and energy terms, not the certificate).
Sub-minute heat bursts are summarized by intra-interval peak statistics in
$w$; hardware frequency scaling (DVFS) remains the ultimate backstop beyond
all modeled sets and appears nowhere else in the model.

The model is discretized exactly (zero-order hold) at $\Delta = 5$\,min for
certification and $30$\,s for simulation. Parameters are calibrated so the
plant reproduces the measured behavior of the production liquid-cooled hall
of~\cite{gheni26} --- supply-temperature relaxation $17 \to 25\,^\circ$C
shedding 63\% of cooling power --- with loop time constants of 5--8\,min,
facility time constant $\sim$50\,min, and junction time constant
$\sim$24\,s.

\subsection{Context}\label{sec:context}
The context vector $c$ collects what is knowable at the day-ahead deadline:
the NWP dew-point forecast $\Tdew^{\mathrm{fc}}$ (which sets the floor
$\vartheta$ and the pre-cooled ready state) and workload-forecast features
(hour-of-day and the operator's running-job state,
Sec.~\ref{sec:cs-setup}). Everything downstream --- the envelope, the
disturbance sets, the offer --- is a function of $c$.

\section{The Certified Flexibility Envelope}\label{sec:envelope}

\subsection{Deliverability as a Polyhedron}
Fix a context $c$, an initial state $x_0$, and the product $(q, d)$ with
$N = \dH/\Delta$ steps and activation window of $m = d/\Delta$ steps. The
commitment is \emph{deliverable} if some admissible input sequence keeps the
plant safe and meets both settlement clauses of Sec.~\ref{sec:settlement}:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{D}(c) = \Big\{ (x_0, q, u_{0:N-1}) :\;
& x_{t+1} = A x_t + B u_t + E \bar Q, \notag\\
& u_t \in \mathcal{U}(x_t; c),\quad
  T_{\mathrm{j},t} \le T^{\max},\; T_{\mathrm{f},t} \le T_{\mathrm{f}}^{\max},
  \notag\\
& P(u_t) \le P^{\mathrm{b}} - q, \quad t < m, \label{eq:window}\\
& \textstyle\sum_{t<N} \big(P^{\mathrm{b}} - P(u_t)\big)\Delta \ge
  r q \dH . \label{eq:hour}
\end{align}
Because the dynamics are linear, $\mathcal{U}$ is affine in $x$, and $P$ is
affine in $u$, eliminating the states gives a polyhedron
$G z \le h(c)$ in the lifted variable $z = (x_0, q, u_{0:N-1})$, affine in
the context through $\vartheta(c)$. Constraint~\eqref{eq:window} is the
physical promise (depth during the window); \eqref{eq:hour} is the settled
promise (energy over the hour). The pair is not redundant: in-hour recovery
makes \eqref{eq:window} alone anti-conservative, and \eqref{eq:hour} alone
would let depth be served by shallow long reductions.

\begin{theorem}[Envelope]\label{thm:envelope}
For each context $c$, the deliverable set $\mathcal{D}(c)$ is a polyhedron in
$(x_0, q, u)$, and the \emph{flexibility envelope}
$F(x, c) = \max\{q : (x, q, u) \in \mathcal{D}(c) \text{ for some } u\}$
is concave and piecewise affine in $x$, nonincreasing in $\Tdew$, and
nonincreasing in $d$. Its projections onto any two coordinates of $(x_0, q)$
are computable exactly.
\end{theorem}

\emph{Proof sketch.} Polyhedrality is by construction; concavity and
piecewise affinity follow because $F$ is the support-function-like optimal
value of an LP whose feasible set is a polyhedron in $(x,q)$; monotonicity in
$\Tdew$ because $\vartheta$ enters only as a right-hand side that tightens
with $\Tdew$; monotonicity in $d$ because the window constraint set grows
with $m$. Exact projections follow from recursive expansion of the
polyhedron's shadow \cite[cf.][]{vrettos16tps}; we compute them with the
Bretl--Lall algorithm. In cross-validation, $10^3$ random lifted points
reproduce membership in the projected envelope with zero anti-conservative
errors. \hfill$\blacksquare$

Quantitatively, the calibrated hall sustains 53--59\% of baseline cooling
power for $d = 30$\,min from the pre-cooled ready state on a dry day --- the
thermal mass is a genuine half-hour resource --- and the envelope collapses
toward zero as the dew point approaches the supply floor
(Fig.~\ref{fig:F1}(b)).

\subsection{Weather-Coupled Virtual Battery}
Projecting $\mathcal{D}(c)$ onto aggregate power-energy coordinates yields a
virtual battery~\cite{hao15} whose parameters are \emph{functions of
context}: capacity
$E_{\mathrm{cap}}(c) = \sum_i C_i\,\overline{\Delta T_i}(c)$ (the thermal
mass times the context-dependent admissible temperature swing, e.g.\
45\,kWh per MW$_{\mathrm{IT}}$ on a dry day, shrinking with $\Tdew$), power
limits set by $\mathcal{U}(x;c)$, and leakage set by the loop's heat-exchange
rates. Closed forms match the LP envelope within 3\% across the context grid.
The object ``a battery whose capacity depends on the weather'' is, we
believe, the right mental model for thermal flexibility, and it is what an
aggregator should be handed.

\subsection{Readiness and Commitment Spacing}\label{sec:readiness}
Define the startable set $\mathcal{R}(q) = \{x : F(x,c) \ge q\}$. Under
whole-hour settlement, the post-delivery state never lies in
$\mathcal{R}(q)$ for full-depth commitments --- \emph{consecutive} full-depth
delivery is thermodynamically impossible, because the recovery energy of hour
$h$ cancels the delivered energy of hour $h{+}1$. We therefore impose
commitment spacing (no two adjacent committed hours) at the offering stage,
which reserves an uncommitted hour in which a recovery law restores the ready
state (Lemma~\ref{lem:e0}). This is a structural property of energy-settled
thermal products, not an artifact: any thermal-inertia resource settled on
hourly energy faces it.

\section{Two-Stage Offering with Delivery Guarantees}\label{sec:offering}

\subsection{Nested Context-Conditional Disturbance Sets}
Hourly disturbance records are summarized by three physically chosen
statistics: the peak step residual $\max_t w_t$, the positive residual energy
$\sum_t [w_t]_+ \Delta$, and the dew-point forecast residual. For each
context, a $k$-nearest-neighbor split-conformal calibration~\cite{lei18}
yields a \emph{budget polytope}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{W}_\varepsilon(c) = \Big\{ w : \max_t w_t \le \bar w(c),\;
\textstyle\sum_t [w_t]_+ \Delta \le \bar E(c) \Big\}
\times \{ |\delta_{\mathrm{dew}}| \le \bar D(c) \},
\label{eq:box}
\end{equation}
with the miscoverage budget $\varepsilon$ allocated across the three faces a
priori (any allocation summing to $\varepsilon$ is valid by the union bound),
so that $\Pr\{ w \in \mathcal{W}_\varepsilon(c) \} \ge 1 - \varepsilon$
marginally, conditionally on the context neighborhood. The budget (energy)
face is essential: against bursty traces, an amplitude-only box is
catastrophically conservative, while the budget face credits the fact that a
burst cannot persist.

The two clauses of the delivery problem have different stakes, so they get
\emph{different} sets:
a \textbf{safety set} $\Wsafe(c) = \mathcal{W}_{\epss}(c)$ with small, fixed
$\epss$ (we use $0.05$), which is never traded for revenue; and a
\textbf{delivery set} $\Wdel(c) = \mathcal{W}_{\epsd}(c) \subseteq \Wsafe(c)$
whose level $\epsd$ is a \emph{product design variable}: delivery failures
beyond $\Wdel$ are priced by the $\gamma$-penalty of
Sec.~\ref{sec:settlement}, so $\epsd$ trades certified depth against expected
penalty, and the offering objective internalizes the trade. Beyond $\Wsafe$
--- a sub-$\epss$-probability event --- thermal excursions are bounded by the
plant's passive dynamics and hardware frequency scaling; the case study
measures them (Sec.~\ref{sec:cs-cert}).

\subsection{Tube Tightening and the Fallback Certificate}
Fix a stabilizing gain $K$ (an off-line LQR design on the discretized plant)
and the \emph{fallback policy} $u_t = \bar u_t + K (x_t - \bar x_t)$ around a
nominal plan $(\bar x, \bar u)$. The error $e_t = x_t - \bar x_t$ obeys
$e_{t+1} = (A + BK) e_t + E w_t$ with $e_0$ in a pre-positioning ball
(Lemma~\ref{lem:e0}). Worst-case error margins
$M_t = \max_{w \in \mathcal{W}(c)} |e_t|$ (componentwise) admit an exact
greedy evaluation for budget polytopes --- sort the impulse-response
coefficients of $(A{+}BK, E)$ and fill the energy budget largest-first ---
plus the decayed initial-error term $|(A{+}BK)^t|\,\bar e_0$. Tightening each
row of $G z \le h(c)$ by the margin of the states and inputs it touches,
\emph{with safety rows tightened by the $\Wsafe$-margins and the
delivery rows \eqref{eq:window}--\eqref{eq:hour} by the $\Wdel$-margins},
yields the \textbf{tightened envelope} $\Ft(x, c) \le F(x, c)$.

\begin{lemma}[Pre-positioning]\label{lem:e0}
Let the uncommitted hour preceding an obligation run the recovery law
$u = \bar Q + K_{\mathrm{rec}} (x - x^{\mathrm{ready}})$ elementwise on the
loop states. Then the start-of-hour error satisfies
$|e_0| \le \rho^{N} |e_{\mathrm{init}}| + \bar w_{\mathrm{typ}} /
K_{\mathrm{rec}}$ componentwise, where $\rho < 1$ is the closed-loop step
contraction. With the calibrated plant and
$K_{\mathrm{rec}} = 80$\,kW/K, a worst-case 15\,K post-event excursion
contracts below the disturbance-driven floor within the hour, giving
$\bar e_0 = 1.25$\,K.
\end{lemma}

The disturbance-driven term $\bar w_{\mathrm{typ}}/K_{\mathrm{rec}}$ ---
not the transient --- is binding; certificates built on transient-only
reasoning overstate readiness, and Sec.~\ref{sec:cs-cert} accounts for every
start accordingly.

\begin{theorem}[End-to-end guarantee]\label{thm:main}
Let $q \le \Ft(x^{\mathrm{ready}}, c)$ be committed for hour $h$ with spacing
(Sec.~\ref{sec:readiness}), and let the hour run the fallback policy from
any $x_0$ with $|x_0 - x^{\mathrm{ready}}| \le \bar e_0$. Then:
(i) \emph{safety}: for every $w \in \Wsafe(c)$,
$T_{\mathrm{j},t} \le T^{\max}$ and $T_{\mathrm{f},t} \le
T_{\mathrm{f}}^{\max}$ for all $t$, and the realized inputs remain admissible,
$u_t \in \mathcal{U}(x_t; c)$; hence safety holds with probability at least
$1-\epss$.
(ii) \emph{delivery}: for every $w \in \Wdel(c)$, the settled energy
satisfies $\sum_{t} (P^{\mathrm{b}} - P_t)\Delta \ge r q \dH$ and the window
depth \eqref{eq:window} holds; hence the obligation is met with probability
at least $1-\epsd$, and the expected shortfall penalty is bounded by
$\epsd\, \gamma\, \pi^{\mathrm{cap}} r q \dH$.
\end{theorem}

\emph{Proof sketch.} The margins $M_t, \mu_t = |K| M_t$ upper-bound the
state and input deviations from the nominal plan for all $w$ in the
respective set and all $|e_0| \le \bar e_0$, by the exactness of the support
function of \eqref{eq:box} under the greedy evaluation and the triangle
inequality for the $e_0$ term. Feasibility of the tightened rows therefore
implies feasibility of the original rows for the realized trajectory: safety
rows against $\Wsafe$, delivery rows against $\Wdel \subseteq \Wsafe$ (so the
error bound used by the delivery rows is also valid). The probability
statements follow from the marginal validity of the conformal sets; the
penalty bound from pricing the complement of $\Wdel$ at the worst-case
shortfall. \hfill$\blacksquare$

On the operating day, a rolling controller may improve on the fallback ---
re-solving the suffix LP with depth-indexed margins retains
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} by construction (the fallback is always a feasible
suffix) --- but the guarantee never depends on it: the certificate \emph{is}
the fallback. This inversion --- a deliberately simple real-time layer
underwritten by an offline certificate --- is what makes the scheme
implementable on facility control hardware.

\subsection{Day-Ahead Offering}
With hours decoupled by spacing and the ready state restored between
obligations, the offer separates per hour:
\begin{align}
\max_{0 \le q \le \Ft(x^{\mathrm{ready}}_h, c_h)} \;
& \pi^{\mathrm{cap}}_h q
- p_{\mathrm{act}} \Big[ r q \dH\, \overline{\Delta\pi}^{\mathrm{rt}}_h
+ D_h(q) \Big] \notag \\
& - p_{\mathrm{act}}\, \epsd\, \gamma\, \pi^{\mathrm{cap}}_h r q \dH ,
\label{eq:offer}
\end{align}
where the bracket carries the expected real-time cost of the energy-neutral
recovery shift and the degradation cost $D_h(q)$ evaluated on the nominal
plan, and the last term prices the delivery risk the certificate leaves to
the settlement. The objective is evaluated on a $q$-grid (exact up to grid
resolution since $D_h$ is convex in $q$); commitment spacing is enforced
greedily by expected value. Everything in \eqref{eq:offer} is a function of
the context and the day-ahead forecasts only.

\section{Case Study}\label{sec:casestudy}

\subsection{Data, Calibration, and Protocol}\label{sec:cs-setup}
All experimental inputs are measured data; no synthetic disturbance process
appears anywhere in this section. The plant is calibrated to the
liquid-cooled hall of~\cite{gheni26} (Sec.~\ref{sec:plant}); its parameters
are frozen before any market experiment. Heat-load uncertainty comes from the
Alibaba PAI GPU-cluster trace~\cite{weng22} (2.0\,M workers over 46 usable
days), mapped to a 1\,MW$_{\mathrm{IT}}$ hall profile at 5-min resolution.
Day-ahead heat forecasts use only information available at the 12:00 issue
time: the survival-weighted load of jobs already running plus an
hour-of-day/level regression for short-job churn, both fit causally.
Weather is the KIAH (Houston Intercontinental) ASOS record paired with
\emph{archived} day-ahead numerical-weather-prediction dew-point
forecasts,\footnote{Open-Meteo previous-runs archive,
\texttt{dew\_point\_2m\_previous\_day1}; measured day-ahead residual standard
deviation 2.0\,K at this site. Price and weather data accessed via
\texttt{gridstatus} and the Iowa Environmental Mesonet.} so both the
disturbance-set calibration and every simulated day use real forecast
errors. Prices are ERCOT day-ahead SPPs, 15-min real-time SPPs, and ECRS
clearing prices for the Houston hub.

The protocol separates design from validation. The trace is split into day
blocks: the first two thirds (\emph{fit block}) calibrate forecasts,
conformal sets, and every design constant (feedback gain, face allocation,
$\epsd$); the final third (\emph{evaluation block}) is replayed exactly once,
in closed loop, over ten 2024 ERCOT weeks spanning all seasons (including
Winter Storm Heather) $\times$ 20 seeds. All participants in a seed face
identical disturbances and activation calls. Every figure and table
regenerates from committed configurations and seeds by a single
\texttt{make figures}.

Participants: \textbf{B1} non-participating plant; \textbf{B2} offers from
the deterministic envelope $F$; \textbf{B3} offers from a 20-scenario
sample-approximation box (no guarantee); \textbf{B4} the proposed scheme
($\Ft$, $\epss{=}0.05$, $\epsd{=}0.3$); \textbf{B5} a stationary battery
sized to the same hourly energy; \textbf{B6} workload curtailment priced at a
quality-of-service cost. B2--B4 share the offering objective, settlement, and
real-time machinery; they differ only in the envelope they trust.

\subsection{The Certification Wall and the Value of Information}
\label{sec:cs-wall}
Fig.~\ref{fig:F1}(a) sweeps the certified offer against residual workload
volatility (Borg-2019~\cite{tirmazi20} residuals scaled by $\kappa$; dry day,
hour 14, $d{=}30$\,min). Certification is volatility-limited: the product
holds a $\sim$125\,kW/MW$_{\mathrm{IT}}$ plateau up to $\kappa \approx 0.5$,
then falls through 92 and 50\,kW to zero at the full mixed-batch volatility
$\kappa{=}1$. The binding statistic is not burst amplitude but
\emph{sustained hourly residual energy}: deviations persisting for tens of
minutes consume exactly the buffer the product sells.

The markers place the \emph{real} PAI hall on this wall at two day-ahead
information levels. With a climatology forecast, its conformal sets certify
40.7\,kW; the job-aware forecast --- using only the running-job state the
operator already possesses at the deadline --- shrinks held-out residual
energy by 19\% and lifts the certified offer to 74.6\,kW ($+83\%$). Day-ahead
workload information is not an accuracy nicety: it moves a hall along the
certification wall, and pricing that movement is what a context-dependent
envelope is for. Fig.~\ref{fig:F1}(b) shows the weather coupling: the
conditional envelope degrades smoothly with the NWP dew-point forecast, while
a context-free certificate must assume the worst hour and forfeits most
dry-day capability.

\subsection{Ten-Week Closed-Loop Market Results}\label{sec:cs-table}
Table~\ref{tab:main} aggregates 1{,}400 controller-days per participant.
Three observations order the comparison.

\emph{(i) Safety is the product.} B2 monetizes the most capacity
(\$35.1/day per MW$_{\mathrm{IT}}$) but violates the hotspot limit on 229 of
1{,}400 day-seeds, by up to $+9.8$\,K --- on real workload weeks, an
uncertified envelope is not a flexibility product but deferred downtime. B4
commits $8\times$ less capacity, monetizes \$10.6/day, and incurs \emph{zero}
violation episodes inside its certified domain; its ten beyond-domain
excursions (statistically expected: the safety set is calibrated at
$1-\epss = 95\%$) stay below $1.6$\,K --- where frequency scaling is the
designed backstop --- versus B2's $9.8$\,K. B3 lands between (47 violation
day-seeds, up to $4.9$\,K): the gap is the guarantee, not the stochastic
modeling.

\emph{(ii) Value concentrates where the grid needs it.} The scarcity week
carries the economics: B4 commits 414\,kW/day and earns \$97/day per
MW$_{\mathrm{IT}}$ during Winter Storm Heather, near the ceiling that even
the unconstrained B2 attains. Averaged across all ten weeks the certified
value is \$10.6/day ($\approx$\$3.9k/MW-yr): 2024 ERCOT capacity prices
outside scarcity are a few \$/MWh, so the ceiling for \emph{any} strategy is
B2's \$35. Cooling-side flexibility is a scarcity asset --- the correct shape
for a resource whose marginal cost is thermal risk.

\emph{(iii) Zero capex, zero workload impact.} Scaled to a 100\,MW campus,
the certified product is worth $\approx$\$390k/yr using hardware the
facility already owns. The battery returns less (\$6.5/day) before its
capital cost is counted; curtailment returns more (\$25.6/day) but prices
direct interference with the compute product, which the cooling side never
touches. They are portfolio complements; only the cooling loop is free.

\subsection{Certificate Validation}\label{sec:cs-cert}
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} makes two falsifiable claims; the held-out replay
tests both at the design point $\epsd = 0.3$. \emph{Safety:} across 1{,}400
day-seeds, no violation episode starts in an hour whose realized disturbance
lies in $\Wsafe(c)$ --- zero in-domain episodes, matching clause (i).
\emph{Delivery:} of 122 activated obligations, 24 fell short (19.7\%, within
the priced budget $\epsd = 0.3$); every failure is attributable to a warm
start at the envelope boundary covered by Lemma~\ref{lem:e0}'s ball, and none
occurred on a cold start with in-set disturbances (0 of 22; exact 95\%
Clopper--Pearson upper bound 15.4\%). At the conservative frontier point
$\epsd = 0.1$, the observed rate is 12.7\% against the larger set, with value
\$9.1/day: the $\epsd$ frontier trades certified depth against priced
delivery risk, and the settlement internalizes the trade. Three adversarial
stress days designed \emph{outside} every calibrated set (all-hours
top-decile bursts, $+3$\,K systematic dew shift, six consecutive activations)
bound the failure mode: B4's worst excursion is $2.2$\,K with penalties of a
few \$/day, versus B2's $5.1$--$6.9$\,K.

\subsection{Degradation-Cost Sensitivity}\label{sec:cs-f3}
Fig.~\ref{fig:F3} sweeps the lifetime-cost coefficient $c_{\mathrm{deg}}$
over two orders of magnitude. The certified commitment contracts smoothly
from 1{,}368 to 498\,kW on the scarcity day (value \$867 $\to$ \$445/day) and
exits entirely on the mild day beyond
$c_{\mathrm{deg}} \approx 20$\,\$/K$\cdot$h: an operator who prices silicon
wear conservatively self-selects out of low-price hours but remains a
scarcity resource. The supply curve an aggregator sees is monotone, smooth,
and price-elastic at exactly the hours the grid values least.

\section{Limitations and Outlook}\label{sec:limits}
Four modeling commitments bound the claims. (i) The product duration $d$ is a
parameter, not a variable: thermal inertia is a minutes-scale resource, and
the envelope quantifies exactly how capability decays with $d$ --- products
requiring hours belong to batteries and workload shifting. (ii) The topology
assumes chiller-assisted heat rejection, so reducing rejection power is
always feasible; free-cooling-dominated plants would couple the envelope to
ambient temperature through a different mechanism. (iii) Metering is at the
cooling plant against an exogenous baseline (Remark~\ref{rem:baseline}).
(iv) Beyond the calibrated safety set, hardware frequency scaling is the
backstop; the case study measures those excursions ($\le 1.6$\,K at
$1-\epss = 95\%$ coverage) rather than assuming them away. Economically,
average value is price-limited --- the certified product captures a third of
the unconstrained ceiling on 2024 ERCOT prices and near-parity in scarcity
--- so its business case is scarcity participation plus interconnection
optionality~\cite{norris25,sb6}, not baseload arbitrage. The clearest paths
to deeper certified offers are informational and structural: operator job
schedules (our running-job forecast is a lower bound on what operators know),
duration-layered products ($d{=}15$ alongside $d{=}30$), and affine
disturbance feedback in place of the fixed gain.
